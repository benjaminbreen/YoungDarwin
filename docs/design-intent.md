# Design Intent

Young Darwin 3D is a playable historical simulation by Benjamin Breen, a
historian of science and medicine. The project should work both as a game and as
a teaching object for history of science courses.

## Core Aim

The game is an argument about field science as embodied, local, uncertain, and
ecologically entangled. Darwin should not feel like a finished icon collecting
facts on rails. He should feel like a young naturalist learning through travel,
fatigue, tools, specimens, mistakes, local encounters, and changing ecological
conditions on Floreana / Charles Island.

Animal modes are part of that argument. Finch and tortoise play should make the
same island readable through different bodies, risks, affordances, and timescales.
Darwin becomes one actor in the ecology rather than the only subject of history.

## Educational Use

Ben plans to use the project in history of science classes and to share a
curriculum/syllabus with assignments and assessment materials. The codebase and
content should make that future work easier by exposing clear systems for:

- Observation, evidence, uncertainty, and interpretation.
- Collection, classification, and the costs of scientific fieldwork.
- Place-specific history: Floreana, the Beagle voyage, settlement, labor,
  introduced species, local knowledge, and ecological change.
- Assignable moments that students can analyze, debate, or write about after
  play.

Do not turn the game itself into a quiz, worksheet, or static lecture. Curriculum
can sit beside the game; the game should create concrete experiences that
classes can discuss and assess.

## Play Goal

The game should also be genuinely fun without classroom framing. Prefer compact,
readable loops with good feedback over broad simulation soup:

- Darwin observes, collects, travels, records, and manages risk.
- Finch flies, perches, feeds, evades, and leaves traces.
- Tortoise grazes, rests, braces, drinks, navigates slopes, and leaves traces.

Educational value should emerge from satisfying play: discovery, tension,
orientation, bodily constraints, memorable animals, legible places, and small
consequences that persist across modes.

## Tone

Aim for scholarly, tactile, strange, and playful. Avoid generic survival/crafting
loops, theme-park Darwin, and purely decorative historical flavor. When a feature
can be historically specific, place-specific, or ecologically meaningful, prefer
that over a generic game convention.

## Agent Decision Rules

When adding or changing content, ask:

- Does this make the game more playable or only more informational?
- Does it give instructors something concrete to teach with?
- Does it preserve Floreana / Charles Island specificity?
- Does it surface uncertainty, interpretation, or historical contingency?
- Does it respect animal modes as real perspectives, not novelty skins?
- Can the same feature support fun play now and curriculum/assessment later?
